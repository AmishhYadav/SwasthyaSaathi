# Tuberculosis (TB)

Introduction
Tuberculosis (TB) is a bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
It primarily affects the lungs (pulmonary TB) but can affect other organs.
India accounts for about 27% of global TB cases, making it the highest burden country.

Symptoms
Persistent cough lasting more than 2 weeks
Coughing up blood or sputum
Chest pain
Fever, especially in the evening
Night sweats
Unexplained weight loss
Loss of appetite and fatigue

Causes
Bacteria: Mycobacterium tuberculosis
Transmission: Airborne droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or speaks.
Risk factors: Crowded living, malnutrition, HIV co-infection, diabetes.

Diagnosis
Sputum smear microscopy (AFB staining)
Chest X-ray
GeneXpert / CBNAAT test (rapid molecular diagnosis)
Mantoux tuberculin skin test

Treatment
DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) under RNTCP/NTEP.
First-line drugs: Isoniazid, Rifampin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol (6-9 months course).
Drug-resistant TB: MDR-TB requires second-line drugs for 18-24 months.
Free treatment available at government hospitals under NTEP.

Prevention
BCG vaccination at birth.
Good ventilation, cough hygiene.
Early diagnosis and complete treatment of active cases.
Contact screening of household members.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "TB is hereditary" → False, it is an infectious disease.
❌ "TB only affects poor people" → False, anyone can get TB.
✅ "TB is curable with complete treatment" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
Part of National TB Elimination Programme (NTEP).
Goal: TB-free India by 2025 (Nikshay Poshan Yojana provides nutrition support).

# Typhoid

Introduction
Typhoid fever is a bacterial infection caused by Salmonella typhi.
Spread through contaminated food and water.
Common in areas with poor sanitation, especially during monsoon season in India.

Symptoms
Sustained high fever (stepladder pattern, rising daily)
Headache
Abdominal pain and discomfort
Constipation or diarrhea
Loss of appetite
Weakness and body aches
Rose spots on the chest (in some cases)

Causes
Bacteria: Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi
Transmission: Fecal-oral route — contaminated food, water, or contact with an infected person.

Diagnosis
Widal test (antibody-based, less accurate)
Blood culture (gold standard)
Typhidot test (rapid)

Treatment
Antibiotics: Azithromycin, Ceftriaxone, or Ciprofloxacin (based on sensitivity).
Supportive care: fluids, rest, soft diet.
Hospitalization for severe cases.

Prevention
Drink boiled or purified water.
Eat freshly cooked food, avoid street food during outbreaks.
Typhoid vaccine (injectable or oral) recommended for high-risk areas.
Handwashing with soap before eating and after using the toilet.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "Typhoid spreads through air" → False, it is waterborne/foodborne.
✅ "Boiling water kills typhoid bacteria" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
Typhoid is endemic in India, particularly in urban slums and rural areas.
Improvement in water supply and sanitation is key to prevention.

# Cholera

Introduction
Cholera is an acute diarrheal disease caused by Vibrio cholerae bacteria.
Spreads through contaminated water and food.
Can cause severe dehydration and death within hours if untreated.

Symptoms
Profuse watery diarrhea (rice-water stools)
Severe dehydration
Vomiting
Leg cramps
Rapid heart rate
Low blood pressure
Sunken eyes, dry mouth

Causes
Bacteria: Vibrio cholerae (O1 and O139 serogroups)
Transmission: Contaminated water, raw/undercooked seafood, poor sanitation.

Diagnosis
Stool culture
Rapid diagnostic dipstick tests

Treatment
Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — the cornerstone of treatment.
IV fluids for severe dehydration.
Antibiotics: Doxycycline, Azithromycin (shorten duration).
Zinc supplements for children.

Prevention
Safe drinking water (boiling, chlorination, filtration).
Proper sanitation and handwashing.
Oral cholera vaccine (OCV) in endemic/outbreak areas.
Avoid raw food in outbreak zones.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "Cholera is caused by bad air" → False, it is waterborne.
✅ "ORS saves lives in cholera" → True, it is the most important treatment.

Public Health Context (India)
Outbreaks occur during floods and after natural disasters.
WASH (Water, Sanitation, Hygiene) programs are critical for prevention.

# Diabetes (Type 2)

Introduction
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder where the body becomes resistant to insulin or does not produce enough insulin.
India is known as the "Diabetes Capital of the World" with over 100 million diabetics.

Symptoms
Increased thirst (polydipsia)
Frequent urination (polyuria)
Unexplained weight loss
Fatigue and weakness
Blurred vision
Slow wound healing
Tingling or numbness in hands/feet
Frequent infections (skin, urinary)

Causes
Risk factors: Obesity, sedentary lifestyle, family history, unhealthy diet.
Insulin resistance leading to high blood glucose levels.

Diagnosis
Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG) ≥ 126 mg/dL
HbA1c ≥ 6.5%
Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) ≥ 200 mg/dL at 2 hours
Random Blood Glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL with symptoms

Treatment
Lifestyle changes: Regular exercise (150 min/week), healthy diet, weight management.
Medications: Metformin (first-line), Sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, SGLT2 inhibitors.
Insulin therapy for advanced cases.
Regular monitoring of blood glucose, HbA1c, kidney function, eye exams.

Prevention
Maintain a healthy weight.
Regular physical activity.
Balanced diet rich in fiber, low in refined sugar and processed foods.
Regular health check-ups after age 30.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "Diabetes is caused by eating too much sugar" → Partially true, but obesity and genetics are major factors.
❌ "Only old people get diabetes" → False, Type 2 is now common in young adults and children.
✅ "Regular exercise helps control blood sugar" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
Part of National Programme for Prevention and Control of Cancer, Diabetes, CVD and Stroke (NPCDCS).
India has a high prevalence of diabetes, especially in urban areas.

# Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

Introduction
Hypertension is a chronic condition where blood pressure in the arteries is persistently elevated.
Known as the "silent killer" because it often has no symptoms until complications develop.

Symptoms
Most people have no symptoms (silent condition).
Severe cases: Headache, dizziness, blurred vision, chest pain, shortness of breath.
Nosebleeds (in rare, severe cases).

Causes
Primary (Essential): No identifiable cause; related to genetics, age, lifestyle.
Secondary: Kidney disease, hormonal disorders, medications.
Risk factors: High salt diet, obesity, stress, alcohol, smoking, sedentary lifestyle.

Diagnosis
Blood pressure measurement ≥ 140/90 mmHg on two separate occasions.
Normal: < 120/80 mmHg
Elevated: 120-129 / < 80 mmHg
Stage 1: 130-139 / 80-89 mmHg
Stage 2: ≥ 140/≥ 90 mmHg

Treatment
Lifestyle: Reduce salt (< 5 g/day), regular exercise, weight loss, stop smoking.
Medications: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, Calcium channel blockers, Diuretics, Beta-blockers.
Regular monitoring and medication compliance.

Prevention
Low-salt diet (DASH diet recommended).
Regular physical activity (30 min/day).
Limit alcohol, quit smoking.
Stress management (yoga, meditation).
Regular blood pressure checks.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "Only stressed people get hypertension" → False, many causes exist.
❌ "If I feel fine, my BP must be normal" → False, hypertension is often asymptomatic.
✅ "Reducing salt intake helps lower blood pressure" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
Hypertension affects 1 in 4 adults in India.
Major contributor to heart attacks, strokes, and kidney disease.
NPCDCS programme covers screening and management.

# COVID-19

Introduction
COVID-19 is a respiratory illness caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus.
First identified in Wuhan, China, in December 2019.
Declared a global pandemic by WHO in March 2020.

Symptoms
Fever, cough, sore throat
Shortness of breath
Fatigue, body aches
Loss of taste or smell (anosmia)
Headache, diarrhea (in some cases)
Severe cases: Pneumonia, ARDS, organ failure

Causes
Virus: SARS-CoV-2 (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2)
Transmission: Respiratory droplets, aerosols, contact with contaminated surfaces.

Diagnosis
RT-PCR test (gold standard)
Rapid Antigen Test (RAT)
CT scan for lung involvement

Treatment
Mild cases: Home isolation, rest, paracetamol, hydration.
Moderate: Oxygen therapy, steroids (Dexamethasone), anticoagulants.
Severe: ICU admission, mechanical ventilation, Remdesivir, Tocilizumab.
Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure.

Prevention
Vaccination (Covishield, Covaxin, and others).
Wear masks in crowded places.
Hand hygiene and social distancing.
Good ventilation in indoor spaces.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "COVID-19 is just like the flu" → False, it can be much more severe.
❌ "5G causes COVID-19" → Completely false.
✅ "Vaccines significantly reduce severe disease and death" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
India conducted one of the world's largest vaccination drives.
CoWIN platform used for registration and tracking.

# Diarrheal Disease

Introduction
Diarrheal disease is characterized by the passage of three or more loose or liquid stools per day.
A leading cause of death in children under 5, especially in developing countries.
Caused by a variety of bacteria, viruses, and parasites.

Symptoms
Frequent loose or watery stools
Abdominal cramps or pain
Nausea and vomiting
Fever (in some cases)
Signs of dehydration: dry mouth, reduced urination, sunken eyes, lethargy

Causes
Bacterial: E. coli, Salmonella, Shigella, Vibrio cholerae
Viral: Rotavirus, Norovirus
Parasitic: Giardia, Entamoeba histolytica
Transmission: Contaminated water and food, poor hygiene.

Diagnosis
Clinical assessment of dehydration
Stool examination (routine and culture)
Electrolyte panel if severe

Treatment
ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) — most critical treatment.
Zinc supplementation for children (10-20 mg/day for 10-14 days).
Continue feeding/breastfeeding.
Antibiotics only if specific bacterial cause identified.
IV fluids for severe dehydration.

Prevention
Safe drinking water.
Handwashing with soap (especially before eating, after toilet).
Rotavirus vaccination for infants.
Proper food hygiene and storage.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "Stop eating and drinking during diarrhea" → False, fluids and food are essential.
❌ "All diarrhea needs antibiotics" → False, most cases are viral and self-limiting.
✅ "ORS + Zinc is the best treatment for childhood diarrhea" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
Part of Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme (IDSP).
ORS and Zinc are available free at all government health facilities.

# Pneumonia

Introduction
Pneumonia is an infection of the lungs that causes inflammation and fluid buildup in the air sacs.
A major cause of death in children under 5 and elderly adults.

Symptoms
Cough with phlegm (yellow, green, or bloody)
High fever with chills
Shortness of breath
Chest pain (worse with breathing or coughing)
Fatigue and weakness
Rapid breathing in children

Causes
Bacterial: Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common), Haemophilus influenzae
Viral: Influenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2
Fungal: Rare, seen in immunocompromised patients
Risk factors: Age (very young or elderly), smoking, chronic lung disease, weakened immunity.

Diagnosis
Chest X-ray
Sputum culture
Blood tests (CBC, CRP)
Pulse oximetry (oxygen saturation)

Treatment
Bacterial: Antibiotics (Amoxicillin, Azithromycin, or Ceftriaxone).
Viral: Supportive care, antiviral drugs if specific virus identified.
Supportive: Oxygen therapy, fluids, rest.
Severe cases: Hospitalization, IV antibiotics, mechanical ventilation.

Prevention
Pneumococcal vaccine (PCV13 for children, PPSV23 for adults).
Influenza vaccination annually.
Good hygiene, handwashing.
Avoid smoking.
Breastfeeding protects infants.

Myths vs Facts
❌ "Pneumonia is just a bad cold" → False, it can be life-threatening.
✅ "Vaccination can prevent many types of pneumonia" → True.

Public Health Context (India)
Pneumonia is the leading infectious cause of death in children under 5 in India.
PCV is part of the Universal Immunization Programme (UIP).
